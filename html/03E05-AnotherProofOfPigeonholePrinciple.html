another proof of pigeonhole principle


By induction on n. It is harmless to let n = m+1, since 0 lacks proper subsets. Suppose that f:n→n is injective.

To begin, note that m∈f⁢[n]. Otherwise, f⁢[m]⊆m, so that by the induction hypothesis, f⁢[m]=m. Then f⁢[n]=f⁢[m], since f⁢[n]⊆m. Therefore, for some k<m, f⁢(k)=f⁢(m).

Let g:f⁢[n]→f⁢[n] transpose m and f⁢(m). Then h|m:m→m is injective, where h=g∘f. By the induction hypothesis, h|m⁢[m]=m. Therefore:

f⁢[n] =g∘h⁢[n]
=h⁢[n]
=m∪{m}
=m+1
=n⁢.
