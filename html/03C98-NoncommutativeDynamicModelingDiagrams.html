0.3 Simple and super‚Äìcomplex dynamics: Closed vs. open systems

In an early report (Baianu and Marinescu, 1968), the possibility of formulating a super‚Äìcategorical unitary theory of systems (that is, of both simple and complex systems, etc.) was pointed out both in terms of organizational structure and dynamics. Furthermore, it was proposed that the formulation of any model or computer simulation of a complex system‚Äì such as living organism or a society‚Äìinvolves generating a first‚Äìstage logical model (not-necessarily Boolean!), followed by a mathematical one, complete with structure (Baianu, 1970). Then, it was pointed out that such a modeling process involves a diagram containing the complex system, (CS) and its dynamics, a corresponding, initial logical model, L, ‚Äòencoding‚Äô the essential dynamic and/or structural properties of CS, and a detailed, structured mathematical model ùêå; this initial modeling diagram may or may not be commutative, and the modeling can be iterated through modifications of L, and/or ùêå, until an acceptable agreement is achieved between the behaviour of the model and that of the natural, complex system (Baianu and Marinescu, 1968; Comoroshan and Baianu, 1969). Such an iterative modeling process may ultimately converge to appropriate models of the complex system, and perhaps a best possible model could be attained as the categorical colimit of the directed family of diagrams generated through such a modelling process. The possible models ùêã, or especially ùêå, were not considered to be necessarily either numerical or recursively computable (that is, with an algorithm or software program) by a digital computer (Baianu, 1971b, 1986-87). The mathematician John von Neumann regarded and defined complexity as a measurable property of natural systems below the threshold of which systems behave ‚Äòsimply‚Äô, but above which they evolve, reproduce, self‚Äìorganize, etc. It was claimed that any ‚Äònatural‚Äô system fits this profile. But the classical assumption that natural systems are simple, or ‚Äòmechanistic‚Äô, is too restrictive since ‚Äòsimple‚Äô is applicable only to machines, closed physicochemical systems, computers, or any system that is recursively computable. Rosen (1987) proposed a major refinement of these ideas about complexity by a more exact classification between ‚Äòsimple‚Äô and ‚Äòcomplex‚Äô. Simple systems can be characterized through representations which admit maximal models, and can be therefore re‚Äìassimilated via a hierarchy of informational levels. Besides, the duality between dynamical systems and states is also a characteristic of such simple dynamical systems. Complex systems do not admit any maximal model. On the other hand, an ultra-complex system‚Äì as applied to psychological‚Äìsociological structures‚Äì can be described in terms of variable categories or structures, and thus cannot be reasonably represented by a fixed state space for its entire lifespan. Simulations by limiting dynamical approximations lead to increasing system ‚Äòerrors‚Äô. Just as for simple systems, both super‚Äìcomplex and ultra-complex systems admit their own orders of causation, but the latter two types are different from the first‚Äìby inclusion rather than exclusion‚Äì of the mechanisms that control simple dynamical systems.
